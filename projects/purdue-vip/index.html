Stroke Rehab Wearable Tracking

This project explored low-cost rehabilitation tracking hardware for measuring arm and hand motion. We started with wearable motion tracking concepts, then expanded into a string-and-encoder approach to capture finger motion. The long-term goal is to turn inexpensive sensor data into useful movement metrics that support therapy progress tracking.

Fusion 360 Sensing & Prototyping Data → Motion Mapping
Role
Mechanical design + analysis + prototyping
Goal
Affordable wearable hardware to capture motion in a repeatable, comfortable way

Design Approach

The core design tension was “low cost” versus “useful signal.” Wearables fail if they’re uncomfortable, inconsistent, or too fragile. I focused on mounting stability, smooth mechanics for the sensing linkage, and a design that can be repeatedly donned without changing calibration every time.

Constraints & early decisions
  • Comfort + repeatability: stable placement matters more than perfect aesthetics.
  • Low-cost sensing: targeted affordable encoders/sensors that can still produce meaningful trends.
  • Mechanical robustness: minimized pinch points and fragile joints for real use.

From Sensors to Motion

The sensing approach evolved from general motion tracking to a more explicit mapping: spring-loaded strings mounted to fingers drive encoders, producing a measurable signal that can be mapped to estimated hand pose. The key challenge is consistency — ensuring that repeat wearing doesn’t shift the mapping or introduce drift that overwhelms the rehabilitation trend.

Prototyping & Validation

My focus was mechanical prototyping and analysis: modeling parts in Fusion 360, running static stress checks where needed, and iterating printed components to balance stiffness, weight, and comfort.

Results

  • Prototyped wearable concepts aimed at low-cost motion tracking with repeatable mounting.
  • Modeled and iterated parts in Fusion 360; used static stress checks to balance stiffness and weight.
  • Contributed to a sensing approach that maps encoder data to estimated hand motion.

What I’d Change Next Time

  • Improve mounting repeatability so donning/doffing introduces less calibration drift.
  • Reduce friction and wear in the string routing path to improve long-term signal stability.
  • Build a faster calibration routine to make setup practical for real use.
